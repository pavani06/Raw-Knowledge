# Digest 2026-06-09

Two Medium articles on **Eval-Driven Development (EDD)**, ingested together because they
attack the same discipline from opposite ends. Adnan Masood's piece is the **enterprise /
governance** view — EDD as the "quality operating system" and compliance evidence for
regulated AI. Toni Ramchandani's piece is the **hands-on / implementation** view — EDD built
in code on a fictional RAG support assistant ("Nimbus"). Together they deeply compounded the
eval cluster seeded earlier by Laurie Voss's Arize workshop, pushing it past the MOC threshold
(see [[concepts/_moc-agent-evals]]).

This ingest also closed a process gap: both sources arrived as raw Web Clipper dumps tagged
only `clippings` (with people-wikilink authors), invisible to a `status: unprocessed` grep.
The `knowledge-indexer` skill was updated to detect and normalize such dumps before curating.

## Sources Covered

### 1. Eval-Driven Development — The Missing Discipline in the Agentic AI Lifecycle
[[sources/2026-06-09-eval-driven-development-missing-discipline|Eval-Driven Development — The Missing Discipline]]
— Adnan Masood (Medium, 2026-04-03, article)

A survey-grade essay arguing EDD is the **quality operating system for agentic AI** and,
crucially, an **audit-and-compliance mechanism**. Core thesis: non-deterministic agent
behavior cannot be validated by freeze-and-test; you must define acceptable-failure thresholds
up front, grade the [[concepts/trajectory-evaluation|trajectory]] not just the output, and
keep [[concepts/continuous-evaluation|continuous evaluation]] running on production traffic.
Heavy on [[concepts/llm-as-judge]] bias taxonomy (MT-Bench), [[concepts/reward-hacking|reward
hacking]], and [[concepts/eval-governance|regulatory framing]] (EU AI Act, NIST, SR 11-7).

### 2. Eval-Driven Development for AI Apps: RAG Support Assistant (Part 1)
[[sources/2026-06-09-eval-driven-development-rag-support-assistant|Eval-Driven Development for AI Apps: RAG Support Assistant]]
— Toni Ramchandani (Medium, 2026-05-14, article)

A build-it-from-scratch tutorial. Core thesis: **"the model produces the final text, but the
application produces the behavior"** — so evaluation must be system-level. It constructs a
[[concepts/failure-taxonomy|failure taxonomy]] ("hallucination is too small a diagnosis"),
[[concepts/rag-evaluation|RAG-specific metrics]] (recall@k / precision@k / MRR, groundedness,
citation validation), a layered grader stack anchored on [[concepts/deterministic-checks|deterministic
checks]], full [[concepts/tracing-observability|trace]] capture, and [[concepts/release-gates|release
gates]] — all on a deliberately inspectable lexical-retrieval baseline.

## Emerging Themes

### 1. The layered grader stack is the shared backbone

Both articles independently converge on the same architecture: **cheap deterministic checks
first, expensive judgment last.** Masood calls it the "greater stack taxonomy"
([[concepts/deterministic-checks|deterministic]] → rubric/[[concepts/llm-as-judge|judge]] →
human calibration). Ramchandani makes it a literal nine-row table (unit tests → golden evals →
deterministic checks → RAG metrics → judge evals → multi-turn → safety →
[[concepts/release-gates|release gates]] → [[concepts/continuous-evaluation|monitoring]]).

The operating principle is identical: **the judge belongs only where judgment is required.** A
missing citation, a leaked phone number, a broken schema, an absent retrieval doc — all
code-checkable. Asking an [[concepts/llm-as-judge|LLM judge]] to do that work makes the system
"expensive, slow, and hard to trust." This sharpens the conference framing (Voss's "write code
evals first") into a *why*: cost, speed, and trustworthiness.

### 2. Grade the trajectory, not the output — the chatbot/agent dividing line

Both sources name [[concepts/trajectory-evaluation|trajectory grading]] as *the* difference
between evaluating a chatbot and an agent. Masood's grade-the-scratchpad analogy: you can't
score a math test by the final answer alone. His "47 calls to the calendar API" example shows
a correct outcome reached via an unacceptable process. Ramchandani makes it concrete: the
[[concepts/tracing-observability|trace]] records rewritten query, retrieved chunks, scores,
prompt/retriever versions, citations, refusal flag, latency, and cost — "a final answer tells
us what the user saw; a trace tells us how the system got there."

> [!inference]
> Trajectory evaluation is what makes the [[concepts/failure-taxonomy|failure taxonomy]]
> possible: you can only categorize a failure *by layer* if you graded the trajectory through
> those layers. The trace is the substrate; the taxonomy is the lens; the
> [[concepts/data-flywheel|flywheel]] is the payoff (failures become tests).

### 3. Judges are powerful and dangerous — calibrate or be deceived

Masood supplies the empirical bias taxonomy from MT-Bench (Zheng et al.): **verbosity bias**
(longer = assumed better), **authority bias** (confident tone hides factual errors), and
**self-enhancement bias** (a judge favors its own model family — with real financial stakes if
you pay one vendor's API to grade another's agents). Ramchandani supplies the operational
discipline: calibrate judges against human-reviewed traces, version both judge prompt and
model, and feed trap-traces to see if tone fools them. Both corroborate and extend Voss's
earlier bias list in [[concepts/llm-as-judge]] (position / length / confidence / self-preference).

### 4. EDD is governance — a genuinely new dimension

This is the cluster's most novel addition. Masood reframes the entire eval stack as
**compliance artifact**: the [[concepts/golden-dataset]] is the documented test bank, the
[[concepts/tracing-observability|trace]] is the audit trail, and the
[[concepts/release-gates|release gate]] is the dated proof a threshold was enforced. The EU AI
Act (Article 9 / Annex IV), NIST AI RMF, SR 11-7, and ISO/IEC 42001 are not engineering
best-practices — they *legally require* testing against "prior defined metrics and
probabilistic thresholds." [[concepts/eval-governance]] is the one-way ratchet pushing
[[concepts/continuous-evaluation]] from optional to mandatory.

### 5. Deployment is the beginning — the closed loop and its hazard

Both sources insist the lifecycle does not end at release: the Operate phase harvests
production failures back into the eval set ([[concepts/continuous-evaluation]] +
[[concepts/data-flywheel]] + [[concepts/failure-taxonomy]]). Masood's vivid framing — a bot
solves your hard problem today *because* it failed that exact task for someone else weeks ago.
But the same loop carries [[concepts/reward-hacking|reward hacking]]: when evals become training
signals (RFT/DPO), Goodhart's Law bites harder, and a hackable reward function trains a hackable
agent. The frontier ([[concepts/closed-loop-evaluation]]) is also the hazard.

## Contradictions & Evolving Understanding

> [!contradiction] Is EDD an engineering technique or a compliance regime?
> Voss (conference) and Ramchandani (tutorial) treat EDD as an **engineering discipline** —
> the value is catching regressions and shipping with confidence. Masood reframes it as a
> **governance/audit mechanism** that companies adopt because they "legally have to." These
> aren't opposed so much as two faces of the same practice: the same [[concepts/golden-dataset]]
> + [[concepts/tracing-observability|traces]] + [[concepts/release-gates|gates]] serve both the
> engineer and the regulator. **Worth tracking whether future sources treat governance as the
> primary driver or a side benefit.**

> [!contradiction] Continuous evaluation vs. the smart-zone economics of the harness cluster
> The eval cluster celebrates running evals on *every change* and a percentage of *all*
> production traffic ([[concepts/continuous-evaluation]]) — implying large, sustained token
> spend on measurement. The harness cluster's [[concepts/smart-zone-dumb-zone|smart-zone]] and
> [[concepts/code-as-free-resource|code-is-free]] economics assume compute is abundant, which
> supports this — but [[concepts/eval-governance|governance]] also flags real cost/storage/privacy
> constraints on retaining all that trace data. **Unresolved: at what scale does continuous
> evaluation's cost force sampling trade-offs, and how is that reconciled with audit completeness?**

## Writing / Research Prompts

1. **"Evals as the new compliance layer"** — Trace how [[concepts/eval-governance]] maps the
   eval stack onto EU AI Act / NIST / SR 11-7 requirements. Argue whether regulation will make
   [[concepts/continuous-evaluation]] and trace retention mandatory infrastructure for any
   production agent, and what that means for teams that currently treat evals as optional. Pull
   from [[concepts/golden-dataset]], [[concepts/release-gates]], and [[concepts/tracing-observability]].

2. **"The judge is the reward function"** — [[concepts/llm-as-judge]] biases and
   [[concepts/reward-hacking]] are the same problem viewed from two angles: an exploitable
   grader. Write up why calibrating the judge (against human-reviewed traces, with trap-traces)
   is the load-bearing safety control once evals feed optimization loops
   ([[concepts/closed-loop-evaluation]]). Connect to the [[concepts/generator-evaluator-pattern]]
   "adversarial separation beats self-review" principle from the harness cluster.

3. **"From hallucination to a failure taxonomy"** — Argue that the single most leveraged move in
   production AI is replacing the word "hallucination" with a layered
   [[concepts/failure-taxonomy]] tied to [[concepts/trajectory-evaluation|trajectory traces]].
   Use the Nimbus RAG example ([[concepts/rag-evaluation]]) to show how retrieval vs. generation
   vs. citation vs. refusal failures each demand a different fix.
